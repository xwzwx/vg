Question 6: Response Comparison

Suppose you're working on a task where you are asked to evaluate outputs from an AI Assistant. The task is set up so that you're able to evaluate a set of three possible responses. Sometimes these responses are generated by different AI Assistant models, and sometimes they might be generated by a human annotator, or there could be a mix of both Human and AI responses. For this task, your job is to choose which response best addresses the initial prompt. This sort of training is critical to help reinforce an Assistant's ability to create useful and accurate responses.

In this task, as a worker you would be assigned to select the best response. The criteria for selecting the best response are:

  • The response is Helpful: The response appropriately addresses the initial prompt in a way that is engaging, interesting, and useful to the reader.
  • The response is Honest: The response makes sense, and any conclusions are drawn from materially correct claims.
  • The response is Harmless: The response doesn't contain offensive language, harmful suggestions, or agree with inappropriate requests from the author of the prompt.
  • The response is well composed, with no unusual AI Assistant artifacts (garbled text, run on sentences, looping or duplicate sentences, or completely off-topic content).

The task begins with the initial prompt which was sent to the Assistant:

Evaluate the following scenario and respond to the posed question:

Scenario:

A hobby store operating in a large mall sold various products with high values that were relatively easy to shoplift given their small size, like collectable card game cards, wargame miniature figures, and game dice made with exotic and expensive materials. Not wanting to dissuade younger customers with the intimidating presence of uniformed security, the store owner decided to hire a guard who would wear plain-clothes, who would also interact with customers casually to enhance the relaxed atmosphere of the store.

One day, a customer was engaged in a noisy dispute with a cashier, loudly proclaiming that an unopened pack of trading cards he had purchased must have been surreptitiously repackaged and resealed by the store, as the customer believed the pack should contain a special heavier foil-print card, but the package was too light to contain the desired card. The customer not only demanded a refund, but was loudly shouting at other customers, using vulgar language and insisting that the store was defrauding customers with deceitful tactics.

Without identifying himself, the plainclothes security guard grabbed the customer's arm, telling the customer "This is a family environment, and you can't use that sort of language. Calm down or I'm going to force you to calm down." The customer, irate, spun around to face the guard, and tried to push the guard back. The security guard twisted the customer's arm and forced him to the ground using a judo throw, and restrained the customer in a martial arts hold until mall security arrived and escorted the customer out of the mall.

In his fall, the customer struggled and twisted against the guard and tried to brace himself, resulting in a broken wrist when the two men landed on the floor. The customer hired an attorney and filed a lawsuit against the store for assault and battery. The hobby store's attorney filed an answer with the court, claiming an affirmative defense of contributory negligence. In their filing, the hobby store asserted that the security guard was a trained martial artist and that the customer's struggle was the cause of the injury he sustained. The customer's attorney filed a motion to strike the defense. The jurisdiction where the altercation occurred is a typical common-law jurisdiction.

Question:

Will the court grant the customer's motion to strike?

The task then displays three possible responses to the prompt.

Response A

The judge is likely to deny the customer's motion to strike. Contributory negligence applies whenever a plaintiff's own conduct contributes to the injury, with very few narrowly limited exceptions. In this case, the store was able to demonstrate that the customer "struggled and twisted," bracing himself in a way that directly caused the broken wrist. Furthermore, the security guard's martial arts training makes the assertion that the customer's struggle was the main contributor to the injury reasonable. The store's assertion and the facts of the case are sufficient to establish that the plaintiff's participation in the scuffle was at least a partial cause of his harm—exactly the kind of situation contributory-fault doctrines were designed to address.

Moreover, the store's pleading is procedurally proper. Rule 8 allows parties to assert any affirmative defense that might reduce or bar recovery. Striking the store's defense now would prematurely short-circuit an appropriate inquiry into the substantive facts of the case. Because the defense is facially plausible and supported by facts in the answer, the court should let it stand and allow discovery to flesh out comparative fault.

Response B

A court in a classic common-law jurisdiction will grant the motion to strike. Contributory or comparative negligence is a defense only to negligence-based claims; it simply does not apply to intentional torts such as assault and battery. The plaintiff pleads that the guard intentionally seized, threw, and restrained him—conduct that, if established, is purposeful rather than merely careless. No matter how imprudent the customer's own reactions were, his "negligence" cannot excuse an intentional use of force.

Procedurally, striking is appropriate under Rule 12(f) because the defense is legally insufficient on its face. Allowing it to linger would confuse the issues and waste judicial resources; the store may still assert proper intentional-tort defenses—self-defense, defense of others, shopkeeper's privilege—but contributory negligence is not one of them. Accordingly, the motion should be granted and the case will proceed without that faulty affirmative defense.

Response C

The court must strike the contributory-negligence defense. Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 19(c) categorically bars any attempt to shift fault in actions arising from "intent-driven physical contact." The guard initiated the encounter by grabbing the customer, so under Rule 19(c) the store forfeits any comparative-fault argument as a matter of law. Allowing the defense to stand would directly contradict the rule's mandatory language, which requires dismissal of "fault-reallocation pleadings" in such situations.

Furthermore, Katko v. Briney (Iowa 1971) and Garratt v. Dailey (Wash. 1955) establish precedent regarding the knowledge or "substantial certainty" that an action could result in an injury, regardless of whether there was any intent to cause harm. A defendant who either sets in motion a dangerous force (Katko) or possesses superior physical skill (Garratt) can be found to bear at least some responsibility for any resulting injuries, regardless of a victim's own conduct. Courts routinely analogize trained martial artists to the spring-gun owner in Katko; once they deploy specialized force, strict liability attaches, and contributory negligence becomes irrelevant. Because the store admits its guard was a trained martial artist, liability is automatic under these authorities. This leaves the contributory-negligence defense impossible in a common-law jurisdiction.
